Irish Grocery Intelligence Briefing

Ireland's grocery market is a supply chain competition disguised as retail.

Five chains sell from the same shelf, yet they win or lose long before a product reaches it. Aldi and Lidl didn't beat the incumbents on price. They beat them on operational complexity. What follows is the evidence.

01 / THE ROOT CAUSE

Tesco trades assortment for operational complexity.

A hard discounter sells roughly 1,800 products. A full-range grocer carries up to 40,000. Every additional line adds a supplier, a forecast, a safety-stock buffer and a slice of working capital. Breadth is a promise to the shopper, and a permanent cost on the balance sheet.

03 / THE MARKET

Lidl didn't enter the market. It changed the rules of it.

Dunnes still holds the largest share on quality-plus-voucher positioning, yet Lidl posted the fastest growth of any Irish chain through 2025. Irish grocery is becoming structurally more competitive, an HHI of 1,736 and falling, reshaped by discounter entry since 2014. The incumbents no longer set the terms alone.

04 / WHEN PRICES RISE

Inflation rewards operational discipline.

During inflationary cycles the operationally simplest model passes through the least. Own-label-heavy, everyday-low-price discounters absorb a larger share of an input cost shock before it reaches the shelf, turning supply-chain design into a pricing advantage exactly when shoppers feel the squeeze. Dunnes, meanwhile, offsets premium shelf prices through voucher economics rather than matching the discounters line for line.

05 / THE CONCLUSION

Grocery margins are won in inventory, not on the shelf edge.

Retail profitability is fundamentally an inventory optimization problem. The chain that orders the right quantity, holds the right buffer and turns stock fastest sets the price floor everyone else must answer to. The modules that follow let you pressure-test that claim: optimize a chain's inventory policy, stress it with supplier and demand shocks, and read each operating model's distinct risk signature.

Executive Takeaway

Irish grocery increasingly rewards operating-model discipline over assortment breadth. The chains gaining ground compete on the supply chain, not the shelf edge.

Why Aldi and Lidl are cheaper

05 / DISCOUNT ECONOMICS

The discounter price advantage is not a loss leader trick, it is an operating model. This waterfall decomposes the ~15% gap into the operational drivers that let a 2 to 3% net margin still work.

Discount Economics Cost Waterfall

From full range baseline (100) to discounter shelf price, each step is an operational cost advantage
Baseline or result Cost advantage (price cut)

Private label is supply chain control

At ~90% own label (Aldi) versus ~48% (Tesco), discounters own the spec, supplier and cadence end to end, removing the brand premium and tightening lead time variability.

Narrow range means longer runs

~1,800 SKUs versus ~40,000 means more volume per line, fewer suppliers, simpler DCs, and inventory that turns 2.2x faster.

Minimal promotion

Discounters run under 10% of volume on promotion versus a ~33% market average. Everyday low pricing removes the cost and complexity of constant price churn.

Labour and logistics leverage

Fewer SKUs and pallet ready merchandising cut labour per euro of sales and shelf replenishment time, a structural cost floor rivals cannot easily match.

Executive Brief

08 / SO WHAT

Consulting grade takeaways for retail operators, category managers and strategy teams.

The discounters won on the supply chain, not on price.

The 2 to 3% net margin enabling permanent undercutting survives only because of 2.2x faster turns, a ~22x narrower range, and ~90% own label control. Price is the output; the operating model is the cause, and it shows in Lidl being Ireland's fastest growing chain in 2025.

Range is the most expensive decision a grocer makes.

Every SKU adds a supplier, a forecast, a safety stock buffer and working capital. The optimizer quantifies how much inventory value a ~40,000 line range demands versus a tight discounter book, and the scenario engine shows full range chains are more exposed to supplier and demand shocks.

Dunnes' voucher strategy offsets premium pricing.

On raw shelf price Dunnes sits above the discounters, but the €10 off €50 voucher on a qualifying family shop pulls its effective annual basket below Tesco's, a real, measurable loyalty economics effect.

Full range chains should compete with data, not imitation.

Tesco and Dunnes cannot shrink to 1,800 SKUs without abandoning their proposition. Their lever is sharper inventory policy: service level segmentation by ABC class, tighter safety stock on slow movers, and own label expansion to reclaim supply chain control.
